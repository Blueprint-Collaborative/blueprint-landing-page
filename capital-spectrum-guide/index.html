Capital Spectrum Guide

Not all capital is
the same. Know the spectrum.

Blueprint works across the full range of capital types, from grants to equity. Understanding where each type fits helps community organizations articulate their needs and helps funders deploy with precision.

Capital types

Four types.
One continuous spectrum.

Each capital type has a distinct purpose, risk profile, and repayment structure. Click any card to explore details.

01 — No return expected
Grant

Philanthropic capital given outright to support mission-driven work. No financial return is expected or required.

View details
Common use cases
  • General operating support
  • Capacity building and staff
  • Advocacy and organizing
  • Early-stage program development
Typical funder types
Foundations, DAFs, family offices, government agencies, individual donors
Repayment
None. Capital is gifted outright with no financial return expectation.
02 — Conditional return
Recoverable Grant

Philanthropic capital that becomes repayable only if the project succeeds or a specific milestone is reached. Failure means no obligation.

View details
Common use cases
  • Pre-development capital for real estate or energy
  • Feasibility studies
  • Bridge financing while grants are pending
  • Catalytic first capital for new ventures
Typical funder types
Mission-aligned foundations, PRIs (Program-Related Investments), CDFIs, movement finance funds
Repayment
Repayable only upon project success or a defined trigger. If the project doesn't succeed, the obligation typically falls away.
03 — Fixed return
Loan

Debt capital with defined repayment terms. Often used by revenue-generating organizations that need capital now against future income.

View details
Common use cases
  • Working capital for cooperatives and social enterprises
  • Real estate acquisition or renovation
  • Equipment and infrastructure
  • Revolving credit for CDFIs
Typical funder types
CDFIs, community loan funds, impact-first lenders, foundations deploying PRIs, mission-aligned banks
Repayment
Principal plus interest on a defined schedule. Terms vary widely — from below-market patient loans to market-rate debt.
04 — Variable return
Equity

Ownership-based investment where the funder shares in the upside — and risk — of the organization or venture's growth.

View details
Common use cases
  • Worker cooperative conversions
  • Revenue-sharing arrangements
  • Social enterprise growth capital
  • Community investment vehicles
Typical funder types
Impact venture funds, community investment funds, mission-aligned family offices, worker ownership funds
Repayment
Returns tied to growth, dividends, or exit. May include non-traditional structures like revenue sharing or community returns.

Capital types exist on a continuum — and can be blended.

In practice, many community organizations need a blend. A land trust might use a grant for operating costs, a recoverable grant for pre-development, and a loan to close the acquisition. Blueprint helps funders and organizations coordinate across the full range so that no capital type has to do a job it wasn't designed for.

Organizing capital needs

Two categories.
Clearer fundraising.

Blueprint organizes capital needs into two categories to help funders understand how and where their capital will be deployed.

Category one
Operational Needs

Capital that keeps the organization running — the staff, infrastructure, and overhead that make the work possible. This is often the hardest capital to raise, and the most critical.

  • Staff salaries and benefits
  • Rent and facilities
  • Technology and communications
  • Leadership development and training
  • Administrative infrastructure
Category two
Project Needs

Capital tied to a specific initiative, asset, or time-limited effort. Project capital often has clearer deliverables and is more likely to trigger recoverable or returnable structures.

  • Real estate acquisition or development
  • Energy system installation
  • Program launch or pilot
  • Research and feasibility studies
  • Capital equipment and infrastructure
Why separate them? Separating operational and project needs — even when they overlap — helps funders understand exactly where their capital lands. Blueprint makes this distinction optional but strongly encouraged, because clarity about deployment builds funder confidence.
